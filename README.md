## EDUCATION AS A POSITIVE EXTERNALITY: GOVERNMENT INTERVENTION 

### ABSTRACT 
This paper explores the role of education as a positive externality and evaluates government intervention outcomes within Nigeria's macroeconomic framework. Using quantitative methods specifically Exploratory Data Analysis of secondary time-series data. It reviews the relationship between public education expenditure, primary school enrollment, and economic growth from 1987 to 2024. The study contrasts the theoretical Pigouvian justification for state subsidies with the practical realities of Nigeria’s chronic underfunding and systemic supplyside barriers. Findings reveal that while absolute nominal spending on education has increased, the expenditure as a percentage of GDP remains chronically low, failing to internalize positive externalities or correct the nation's severe human capital deficit. The paper recommends overhauling spending mechanisms and aligning budgetary allocations with international benchmarks to improve socio-economic spillovers and long-term economic growth in Nigeria.


## ROLE OF INSTITUTIONAL QUALITY IN HEALTH CARE FINANCING AND LIFE EXPECTANCY IN NIGERIA

### Abstract 
This study examines the interactive role of institutional quality in the relationship between health expenditures (capital, recurrent, and out-of-pocket) and life expectancy in Nigeria from 1984 to 2023 using the ARDL approach. Findings indicate that capital health expenditure (CHE) significantly improves life expectancy when analyzed independently (coefficient = 2.647, p = 0.019), but this effect diminishes when institutional quality is introduced, highlighting governance deficiencies. Recurrent health expenditure (RHE) shows no significant impact, reflecting systemic inefficiencies, while out-of-pocket spending (OUE) fails to sustainably enhance life expectancy, exacerbating healthcare inequities. The study underscores that weak institutions undermine health financing effectiveness, necessitating governance reforms to optimize outcomes. Policy recommendations include strengthening institutional frameworks, reforming recurrent expenditure management, reducing reliance on OUE through expanded health insurance, and integrating governance reforms with health financing strategies. These measures are critical for Nigeria to achieve meaningful improvements in population health and life expectancy. 


## EFFECT OF ENTERPRENUERSHIP ON SELECTED ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT INDICATORS IN NIGERIA (1986-2022)

### ABSTRACT
Entrepreneurship development is an indispensable factor in the reduction of poverty and income gap in developing countries where income inequality is high and economic growth is sluggish. Previous studies only focuses on the relationship between entrepreneurship and income inequality or entrepreneurship and economic growth, so this research aims to examine the relationship between entrepreneurship and all the determinants of economic development. This study analysed the trends of entrepreneurship, income inequality, unemployment and economic growth in Nigeria, with a view to examining the effect of entrepreneurship and investigating the direction of causality among entrepreneurship and income inequality, unemployment and economicgrowth in Nigeria. Ex-postfacto research design was used. The studywas anchored on Human Capital Theory by Theodore Schultz in 1960. Secondary data on entrepreneurship, income inequality, unemployment and economic growth from 1986 to 2022 were extracted from sources, namely, World Bank’s Entrepreneurship Database (WBED), Standardized World Income Inequality Database (SWIID), and World Development Indicators (WDI). Data were subjected to descriptive and inferential analyses, using Ordinary Least Square (OLS) and Granger causality tests. The findings showed that there were fluctuations in entrepreneurship, income inequality, unemployment and economic growth, especially in 1990’s due to political instability and changes in governments; from Ordinary Least Square revealed that entrepreneurship has significant negative effect on income inequality and unemployment; and significant positive effect on economic growth in Nigeria. Also, Granger Causality test showed a unidirectional causal relationship from entrepreneurship to unemployment and economic growth, but no causal relationship between entrepreneurship and income inequality in Nigeria. The study thereforeconcluded thatthereisaneedtocreatean enablingenvironment forentrepreneurship to flourish and promote growth, ensure reduction in income inequality and unemployment in Nigeria.
